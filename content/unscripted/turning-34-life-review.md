*Turning 34 this year. Sat down with my own handmade ginger tea and decided to write what actually happened. Not the LinkedIn version. The one with the smell, the rent receipts, the bad days, and the choices nobody noticed.*

---

### Before I Start - A Note To The People Who Say I Talk About Others Because I Cannot Do It Myself

Some people say I am not able to do it, and that is why I talk about others so much. Let me be clear about what I am "not able" to do.

I am not able to lick boots. I am not able to claim somebody else's research as mine. I am not able to put my name on a tool somebody else built and present it on a conference stage. I am not able to walk up to bigshots asking for favours, guidance, and a shortcut into the room. I am not able to play that game, and I am not planning to learn it.

What I am able to do is recognise the real ones when I see them. I have met real hackers in this country - quiet people, the ones who actually break things and actually fix things, the ones who do not need a stage to feel real. I follow quality content from people who do the work. The scenarios I write about, this one included, come from that world, not from the conference photo wall.

So yes, I talk about others. I talk about the real ones because they deserve it, and I talk about the fake ones because somebody has to. If that looks like inability to you, then we are using the word differently.

### The Bike That Became a Laptop

The first turn in my life happened because of an Acer e1531. Intel dual core, 4GB RAM, third gen. I still own it. The keys are shiny in the places my fingers go.

That laptop was supposed to be a bike. My father gave me 30,000 rupees to buy a bike for college. I bought a laptop. He found out later. He did not say much. He gave me 30,000 again, this time for CEH training in Bangalore, and a train ticket.

I think about that a lot. Two times he handed me money he could not really afford to lose, and both times he let me decide what to do with it. He never asked me to be reasonable. He let me be wrong on my own terms. That is a kind of trust I am still trying to earn back by not wasting it.

I am from a farming family. I did not study in English medium. I do not have a degree on the wall. The first English sentence I wrote with confidence was probably an SQL injection payload.

### Room 14, Ramachandra Reddy PG, December 2014

December 2 2014, I moved into Ramachandra Reddy PG, room number 14. Three thousand five hundred a month. December 9 I started CEH at Hackerschool.

The room had bed bugs. I would wake up with rows of bites along my back like someone had stamped a barcode on me. The water came from a tanker. The food at the mess was whatever was cheapest that week. There was a power cut almost every evening, and during the cut you could hear every other room's TV through the walls.

This is where I learned a thing nobody told me before. **Your body is the project. Everything else is a feature on top of the project.** I did not understand that for years. I treated my body like the build server - let it run, restart it when it crashes, do not look at the logs. By the time I started looking at the logs, the server had been failing silently for a long time.

If you are reading this in a similar PG right now, please do this:

- Buy a copper bottle. Boil your water. Do not trust the tank on the roof.
- One real meal a day that you cooked, even if it is just rice and dal in a small cooker.
- Sleep is not optional. The 2am "one more episode" is the most expensive habit a 22 year old can have.
- If your stomach hurts more than two weeks in a row, that is not "Bangalore food." That is your gut sending you a ticket. Open the ticket.

I ignored every one of these for years. I am paying that bill now in lab tests and tablets and food I have to plan around.

### The Printer Repair That Taught Me Everything

After CEH I joined a small firm as Information Security Analyst. Sounded great on the offer letter.

Week two they sent me to a hospital to fix a printer.

I sat on the floor of a corridor that smelled like phenol, with a paper jam in a HP LaserJet, while a nurse stood next to me waiting because the discharge summary would not print. I am supposed to be a security analyst. I am pulling crumpled paper out of a fuser unit. I remember thinking I should walk out. I remember thinking I should call someone and complain. I did neither.

I fixed the printer. I went back to the office. I did not say anything.

Here is the part that is interesting. **That printer taught me more than the offer letter title would have.** Not because of the printer. Because of the hospital. I saw a ward where the patient monitor was running Windows XP. I saw a nursing station where the password was taped under the keyboard. I saw a biomedical engineer plug an unknown USB into a vital signs monitor because the vendor sent it. None of that was on any course I had paid for.

The lesson was not "be humble, do small jobs." The lesson was: **the room you are in is always teaching you something. The job description is just the door. What is inside the room is on you to notice.**

I would not have learned IoT security from a glamorous title. I learned it from a printer in a corridor and the things I saw while I was bored fixing it.

### The First 8000

My first salary in Bangalore was Rs 8000. I remember the deposit message on my Nokia. I read it four times. Nine months earlier I was eating one meal a day and skipping breakfast so the second meal felt bigger.

8000 rupees does not change a life on paper. But that month I bought a cheap external hard disk, paid the PG rent without asking my sister, and ate three meals every day for thirty days in a row. That was the richest I had ever felt. Nothing since has matched that feeling. Not bonuses. Not better jobs. Not international travel.

I think about that a lot too. **The amount of money it takes to make you feel rich the first time is very small. The amount it takes to make you feel rich the second time is infinite.** I try to keep the first number close.

### The Boots I Did Not Lick

I want to put this on record at 34, because by 44 I might forget how hard it was.

I came this far without licking anyone's boots.

I never laughed at a joke I did not find funny just because the manager told it. I never agreed with a technical decision I knew was wrong just to keep the meeting short. I never showed up to a senior's birthday hoping it would translate into a referral later. I never DM'd a famous name with a fake compliment to get noticed. I never let someone else put their name on my research because they had more followers.

I watched other people do all of this. They moved faster than me for years. I sat in my room some nights wondering if I was the slow one, the stubborn one, the one who would still be doing this at 45 with nothing to show.

Then one by one I watched the patrons leave. The manager moved companies. The famous name started a new project and the old followers became invisible to him. The senior whose birthday everyone went to retired and stopped picking up calls. And the people who had built their entire identity on being agreeable were standing in the same place they started, holding a contact list that did not work anymore.

The work I did is still mine. The blog posts I wrote are still online with my name. The community I helped is still here. The slow road turned out to be the only road that did not collapse when the weather changed.

### Arrogance, Self-Respect, and How I Tell Them Apart Now

I used to confuse these two for a long time. I would say something sharp in a meeting and tell myself it was self-respect. It was usually arrogance with better lighting.

The test I use now, after enough mistakes to know:

- **Did I need them to feel small for me to feel okay?** If yes, that was ego.
- **Could I have made the same point with my voice 30 percent quieter?** If yes, the extra volume was ego.
- **Was I correcting the idea, or correcting the person?** If the person, ego.
- **Would I still say this if no one else was watching?** If no, ego.

Self-respect at 34 looks much smaller than I imagined it would at 24. It is mostly saying no without writing a paragraph. Walking out of a chat that turned into a roast. Not replying to the comment that was clearly written to provoke. Not explaining myself to people who were not asking in good faith. Charging what my time is worth even when the client makes a face. Refusing to add my name to research I did not actually do.

It is quiet. It does not announce. It does not need a witness.

### The Diplomatic Trap

There is a trap I see most often, in myself first.

You are in a meeting. Something is wrong with the plan. You can see it. The room is in a hurry to close the slide deck. You feel the math happening - *if I just nod, this ends, I get my evening back.* You nod. The meeting ends. You go home.

That is not diplomacy. That is fatigue wearing a suit.

Real diplomacy is saying the hard thing in a way the other person can actually receive. The shortcut version is silence with a smile. The shortcut feels like peace. It is not. It is interest accruing on a debt you will pay later, when the bad plan ships and the team looks at you and says "you knew, why did you not say anything."

I did this for years. I am not above it now. The thing I have learned is to catch the nod before it leaves my face. **Am I about to agree because I agree, or because I want to leave?** If it is the second one I make myself say the awkward sentence. Ten more minutes of awkwardness in the room is cheaper than ten years of being known as the person who never said anything when it mattered.

### What Compounded, What Did Not

Looking back, there are very clear winners and losers in what I invested time in.

**Compounded:**

- Writing things down publicly, even badly. Old blog posts I am embarrassed by today are still bringing me readers.
- Showing up to null meetups when I had nothing to talk about. The community knew my face before they knew my work.
- Helping people who could not pay me back. Six years later they are the ones who introduce me to the right person.
- Saying no slowly and clearly to gigs that did not fit. The "no"s left space for the right "yes" to find me.
- Buying tools I could not afford with money I should have saved. The hardware paid for itself in skill.

**Did not compound:**

- Trying to look impressive on social media. Net zero return after a decade.
- Arguments I won. I cannot remember a single one that improved my life.
- Conferences I attended just to be seen at. Forgotten by everyone including me.
- Networks I joined because someone said I should. Empty Telegram groups in my archive.
- Money saved by skipping meals. Cost me ten years of digestion.

### What I Got Wrong

Some things I want to say plainly because they belong in a 34th birthday review.

I let my health slide for too long. I treated tiredness as proof of effort. It is not. It is proof of a body running out of resources.

I stayed in a few professional situations longer than I should have because I confused loyalty with stuckness. The room was not loyal to me. I was loyal to the room. There is a difference.

I held grudges over small things. I let some friendships drift because of a tone in one message that I read in the worst possible way. I am still trying to fix some of those.

I undercharged for years. Not because I was generous. Because I was scared the client would say no.

I was harsh with people who were trying their best with less than I had. I confused "having standards" with "having no patience." Those are not the same thing.

### Where I Am at 34

I have a body that I am rebuilding slowly. I do not own a flat. I am not a director at a fancy firm. By the metric most LinkedIn profiles use, I have not "made it."

But I sleep with my spine straight. I owe nobody anything. I have not traded my work for a seat at someone else's table. The community that stood by me when I had nothing - null, IoTSRG (the research community I built and the people in it who respect the work), the seniors who made room for me when I had no business being in the room - I still show up for them. The work I have shipped is mine. Every line of every blog. Every talk on every stage. Every CVE with my name. I did not steal it, I did not borrow a famous name to push it, I did not pay for it with anything other than my time.

That is the only review I care about.

### Notes For the Younger Version of Me

If somehow this reaches a 22 year old me sitting in room 14 with bed bug bites on his back, here is what I would say:

- Start drinking clean water now. Not in five years. Now.
- The laptop was the right call. Keep it longer than people tell you to.
- The printer in the hospital is the most important room you will be in this year. Pay attention.
- Do not try to be the smartest in the meeting. Try to be the most honest. The first one fades, the second one compounds.
- Keep the diary. Even when you write only one line a day. Future you will need the timestamps.
- The famous person you are nervous to email is just a person. Email them. The worst case is silence, which is what you have right now anyway.
- Stop holding the grudge from August. It will cost you a friendship that mattered.
- The girl is right about the food. Listen.
- You will be okay. Not because someone saves you. Because you keep showing up, slowly, on your own legs. That is enough.

### One Last Thing Before 34

I am not writing this to inspire anyone. I do not believe in inspiration that lasts longer than a hot cup of tea. I am writing it because at 34 I am old enough to look back and young enough to still change a few things, and putting it on a page makes it harder to lie to myself about what I actually saw.

If you are in your version of room 14 right now - drinking the bad water, eating the bad food, watching faster people pass you, wondering if you are the slow one - you are not. You are the one whose road does not collapse when the weather changes. Stay on it.

Spine straight. Stomach safe. Boots unlicked.

See you at 35.
