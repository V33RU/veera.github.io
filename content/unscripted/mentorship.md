I have been in this space long enough to watch a pattern repeat itself.

Someone talented walks in with raw skill, genuine curiosity, and no connections.
Someone else walks in with confidence, networking ability, and the right vocabulary.

Guess who gets the room more often.

This post is about that. And about the mentors who either fix it - or make it worse.

---

## What Mentorship Actually Means

Mentorship is not a content series.
It is not a LinkedIn endorsement factory.
It is not a podcast feature or a "shoutout to my mentee" post.

Mentorship in security specifically is a transfer of thinking - not just knowledge.

Anyone can teach someone to run a tool.
Anyone can walk someone through a CVE writeup.
Anyone can explain a concept that's already documented.

That is not mentorship. That is tutoring.

**Mentorship is when you teach someone how to think about problems
that don't have a writeup yet.**

It means sitting with someone while they are stuck,
not giving them the answer -
but teaching them the process of finding it themselves.

It means exposing them to failure early,
on safe ground,
so when they fail in public it doesn't break them.

It means making them someone who can stand in a room of experts
and hold the conversation - without you in it.

If your mentee still needs your name to get taken seriously,
the job is not done.

---

## The Benchmark Is Simple

**Are they better than your version of yourself at their stage?**

Not better than you today. Better than you were when you were where they are.

If yes - you are doing it right.
If no - ask yourself whether you are actually investing in them
or investing in how you look by being associated with them.

That is a harder question than it sounds.

---

## The Bootlicker Problem

Let's talk about what nobody wants to say directly.

There is a type of person in security who has optimized for visibility over substance.

They know the right names to drop.
They attend the right conferences.
They have the right opinions at the right times - always aligned with whoever has power in the room.
They collect certifications and conference badges like currency.
They speak loudly about community and contribution
while their actual technical contribution is close to zero.

And they rise. Because the system rewards performance, not depth.

This is not about jealousy.
Jealousy is about wanting what someone else has.
This is about watching something genuinely harmful happen to a field that matters.

When the wrong people get platforms -
they influence curriculum,
they shape what "good" looks like,
they become the face others model themselves after.

And the next generation learns the wrong lesson:
that visibility is the skill.

The researchers doing actual work - finding bugs, building tools,
writing real research at 2am for no applause -
get filtered out by people who are better at the game
than at the craft.

That is the real damage.

---

## Mentors Are Not Neutral in This

Here is the uncomfortable part for people in senior positions.

Every time you publicly endorse someone who hasn't earned it -
you are spending credibility that belongs to the community.

Every time you give a platform to someone polished over someone capable -
you are teaching the next generation what to optimize for.

Every time you protect a mentee from honest feedback
because you don't want to hurt the relationship -
you are sending them into the industry unprepared
and setting them up to fail badly, publicly, later.

Mentors are not neutral. They are either correcting the system or feeding it.

There is no middle position.

---

## What Mature Mentorship in Security Actually Requires

**From the mentor:**

- Enough self-awareness to separate your ego from their growth.
  Their success is not your achievement. It is theirs.
  Your job was to create the conditions for it - not own it.

- The discipline to give feedback that is true, not comfortable.
  If their research is weak, say it clearly and help them fix it.
  A mentor who only validates is a mirror with good lighting - useless.

- Selectivity about who you back publicly.
  Your endorsement should mean something.
  That means not giving it freely.

- The maturity to let them disagree with you -
  and to recognize when they are right.
  If you cannot handle your mentee challenging your thinking,
  you are not mentoring. You are building an audience.

- Long-term thinking over short-term optics.
  The real measure of a mentor shows up 3-5 years later
  when the person is operating independently.
  Not when they post a thank-you on LinkedIn after a workshop.

---

**From the mentee:**

- Understand that the shortcut is the trap.
  The connections, the badges, the name-dropping -
  none of it holds weight when you are alone in front of a real problem.
  Build the skill first. The recognition follows.
  And if it doesn't - the skill still remains.

- Do not confuse access with growth.
  Being in the room is not the same as being ready for the room.
  Use access as exposure, not as a destination.

- Be honest about your gaps.
  A mentor cannot help you fix what you are pretending does not exist.
  The people who grow fastest are the ones who say
  "I don't know this and I need to" - out loud, without embarrassment.

- Choose mentors based on their thinking, not their following.
  The most valuable mentor you will find might have 400 LinkedIn connections
  and no conference appearances.
  Look for depth of thought, not breadth of visibility.

---

## What the Industry Loses

When we get this wrong - when mentorship becomes networking performance
and when the wrong people get amplified -
the damage is not abstract.

We lose researchers who could have found critical vulnerabilities
but got discouraged and went somewhere they were valued.

We lose perspectives that didn't fit the popular mold
but were exactly what a stale space needed.

We produce overconfident people in roles they cannot actually fill,
and they make real decisions - product decisions, architecture decisions,
policy decisions - with skill sets that were never actually tested.

Security is not a field where this is harmless.
The things we defend are real.

---

## The Standard Should Be Higher Here

In most industries, performance theater has limited consequences.
In security, it has a cost that shows up in breach reports.

The bar for who gets mentored, elevated, and platformed
should reflect that.

Not because we are gatekeeping.
Because we are responsible.

---

**Mentorship done right produces people who are harder to break than you were.**

That is the only version worth doing.

---

*If you mentor someone in this field -
hold yourself to this.
Not for optics. Because the work demands it.*
